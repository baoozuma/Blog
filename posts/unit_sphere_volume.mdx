## Motivation

As we know, in the two-dimensional space $\mathbb{R}^2$, the circumference and area of the circle $2 S_1 = (O, r)$ is calculated using the formula

$$
|S_1| = 2\pi r \quad \text{và} \quad [S_1] = \pi r^2.
$$

We also have the sphere's volume and surface area in three dimensions. $2 S_2 = (O, r)$:

$$
|S_2| = 4\pi r^2 \quad \text{và} \quad [S_2] = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3.
$$

Thus, a question arises: how can we calculate the surface area (coarea) and volume of a sphere $S_{n-1}$ in the space $\mathbb{R}^n$ for any $n \in \mathbb{N}$?

---

## Overview of Lebesgue Measure

But first, we need a standard definition of the area or volume of any set. In this article, I will use the Lebesgue measure, other measures can also be calculated in a similar way. Let $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ be the collection of subsets of $\mathbb{R}^n$, *an outer Lebesgue measure* is a function $\lambda^ *: \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R}^n) \to [0, +\infty]$ is defined as follows

$$
\lambda^*(A) = \inf\left\{\sum |R_i| \;\Big|\; \{R_i\} \text{ is a countable collection covering } A\right\}.
$$



A *half cube* $C$ is any Cartesian product of the form $C = \prod_{i=1} ^ n [a_i, b_i)$ with $a_i < b_i$ are real numbers, and its volume is $|C| = \prod_{i=1}^ n (b_i - a_i)$. A set $A$ is called *Lebesgue measurable* if

$$
\lambda^*(E) = \lambda^*(A \cap E) + \lambda^*(E \setminus A), \quad \text{for all } E \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n,
$$

and we write $|A| = \lambda^*(A)$.
   

The properties as well as theorems about Lebesgue measure and integration will be presented in another article. We only need to know that to measure a set, we may approximate outside that set with half cubes whose volume is given by the above formula, and the integrals used in the article are conventionally Riemann integrals. Additionally, to calculate the measure of a surface, we will reduce it to a lower-dimensional space — otherwise, it would yield a measure of $0$, which does not make sense for surface area.    

Now back to the main problem. We observe that when moving any set, its volume (or measure) remains unchanged, or when multiplying the elements of that set by any real number $\alpha$, its measure will increase by a factor of $|\alpha|^n$. This is an invariant property of the Lebesgue measure and is not immediately obvious; it will be proven in detail later. In general, if we know the measure of the unit ball $B(0,1)$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$, we can easily calculate the measure of any other ball. In this article, we will standardize the terminology.

$$
B(0,1) = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid \|x\| < 1\}
\quad \text{và} \quad
\mathbb{S}^{n-1} = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid \|x\| = 1\}.
$$

We see something very interesting that $\mathbb{S}^{n-1}$ is the set of boundary points of $B(0,1)$.

## Necessary tools Necessary tools

Before diving into the calculations, we need to prepare two important results that will appear very naturally in this article.

### Gaussian Integral

 **Lemma.** $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi}$.

**Proof.** Let $I = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}\,dx$. Since $e^{-x^2} > 0$, then $I > 0$. We calculate $I^2$ by writing the product of two independent integrals:

$$
I^2 = \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}\,dx\right)\left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-y^2}\,dy\right) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} e^{-(x^2+y^2)}\,dx\,dy.
$$

This is an integral over $\mathbb{R}^2$ with a function that has circular symmetry, so we switch to polar coordinates $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$ with the Jacobian $r$:   

$$
I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2}\,r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^\infty r e^{-r^2}\,dr.
$$

Let $t = r^2$, then $dt = 2r\,dr$, so

$$
I^2 = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{2}\int_0^\infty e^{-t}\,dt = \pi\cdot\bigl[-e^{-t}\bigr]_0^\infty = \pi.
$$
Because $I > 0$, we have $2 I = \sqrt{\pi}$. $\square$

# The Gamma Function

Another tool that will appear very naturally after a change of variables in the main computation is the Gamma function.

 **Definition.** For $s > 0$, the *Gamma function* is defined by
 $$
 \Gamma(s) = \int_0^\infty t^{s-1} e^{-t}\,dt.
 $$

 **Lemma.** The Gamma function satisfies the following properties:
 1. $\Gamma(1) = 1$.
 2. $\Gamma(s+1) = s\,\Gamma(s)$ for all $s > 0$.
 3. Consequently, for $n \in \mathbb{N}$, $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$
 4. $\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \sqrt{\pi}$.

**Proof.**

*(i)* Direct computation: $\Gamma(1) = \int_0^\infty e^{-t}\,dt = \bigl[-e^{-t}\bigr]_0^\infty = 1$.

*(ii)* Integration by parts with $u = t^s$ and $dv = e^{-t}\,dt$:

$$
\Gamma(s+1) = \int_0^\infty t^s e^{-t}\,dt = \bigl[-t^s e^{-t}\bigr]_0^\infty + s\int_0^\infty t^{s-1}e^{-t}\,dt.
$$

The boundary term vanishes since $t^s e^{-t} \to 0$ as $t \to 0^+$ and $t \to \infty$. Therefore $\Gamma(s+1) = s\,\Gamma(s)$.

*(iii)* From (i) and (ii), induction gives $\Gamma(n+1) = n!$, i.e., $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$.

*(iv)* Substituting $t = x^2$ in the definition,

$$
\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \int_0^\infty t^{-1/2} e^{-t}\,dt = 2\int_0^\infty e^{-x^2}\,dx = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi}.
$$

$\square$

## Computing $|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}|$

Let us compute $|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}|$ first. Consider the $n$-dimensional Gaussian integral

$$
I = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} e^{-\|x\|^2}\,dx.
$$

We compute $I$ in two different ways.

**Method 1 (Fubini).** Using the Gaussian integral computed above:

$$
\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} e^{-\|x\|^2}\,dx = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} e^{-(x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2)}\,dx_1\cdots dx_n = \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}\,dx\right)^n = \pi^{n/2}.
$$

That's it, straightforward.

**Method 2 (Spherical coordinates).** A standard trick for multivariable integrals that resist direct computation is to switch to polar or spherical coordinates, at which point the surface area $|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}|$ appears very naturally. But how does one actually set up spherical coordinates in $\mathbb{R}^n$? Let me walk through it.

### Spherical Coordinates in $\mathbb{R}^n$

Consider the equation $x_1^2 + \cdots + x_n^2 = 1$. Since $-1 \le x_1 \le 1$, we can set $x_1 = \cos\theta_1$. Then we need $x_2^2 + \cdots + x_n^2 = \sin^2\theta_1$. The idea is to keep exploiting the identity $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ and substitute iteratively. Since $-\sin\theta_1 \le x_2 \le \sin\theta_1$, set $x_2 = \sin\theta_1\cos\theta_2$, which gives $x_3^2 + \cdots + x_n^2 = \sin^2\theta_1\sin^2\theta_2$. Continuing in this fashion, for each $1 \le k \le n-1$ we set $x_k = \sin\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{k-1}\cos\theta_k$, yielding the system

$$
\begin{cases}
x_1 = \cos\theta_1 \\
x_2 = \sin\theta_1\cos\theta_2 \\
\quad\vdots \\
x_{n-1} = \sin\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{n-2}\cos\theta_{n-1} \\
x_n = \sin\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{n-2}\sin\theta_{n-1}
\end{cases}
\quad \theta_1,\dots,\theta_{n-2} \in [0,\pi),\quad \theta_{n-1} \in [0,2\pi).
$$

### Jacobian of the Change of Variables

With the $\theta_i$ now arbitrary, define $\sigma(\theta_1,\dots,\theta_{n-1}) = (x_1,\dots,x_n)$. We write $x = r\sigma$. The Jacobian of this change of variables is

$$
J(r\sigma)(r,\theta_1,\dots,\theta_{n-1}) = \det\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial r},\; \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial\theta_1},\;\dots,\; \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial\theta_{n-1}}\end{pmatrix} = r^{n-1}\det\begin{pmatrix}\sigma,\; \dfrac{\partial\sigma}{\partial\theta_1},\;\dots,\; \dfrac{\partial\sigma}{\partial\theta_{n-1}}\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Expanding this determinant directly is quite messy and reveals no useful structure. So instead we use a trick: let $A = \left(\sigma,\; \frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial\theta_1},\;\dots,\; \frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial\theta_{n-1}}\right)$ and compute $A^T A$ rather than expanding $\det A$ head-on.

### Induced Metric and Area Element

When integrating over a hypersurface, the area element $d\sigma$ is defined through the *induced metric* pulled back from the ambient space $\mathbb{R}^n$ via $\sigma$. The tangent vectors of $\sigma$ at a point are $\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial\theta_i}$, and the induced metric $G$ is the $(n-1) \times (n-1)$ matrix with entries

$$
g_{ij} = \left\langle \frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial\theta_i},\, \frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial\theta_j}\right\rangle, \quad 1 \le i,j \le n-1.
$$

This is the Gram matrix of the tangent vectors. Geometrically, $\sqrt{|\det G|}$ measures how much $\sigma$ stretches area when mapping a small patch $d\theta_1\cdots d\theta_{n-1}$ in the parameter domain onto a piece of $\mathbb{S}^{n-1}$. This is why the area element on the sphere takes the form

$$
d\sigma = \sqrt{|\det G|}\; d\theta_1\cdots d\theta_{n-1}.
$$

This formula is a direct consequence of the Riemannian metric and will be discussed more carefully later. For now, let us return to the trick with $A^T A$. Since $|\sigma| = 1$, we have $\langle\sigma, \sigma\rangle = 1$, and differentiating with respect to $\theta_j$ gives

$$
\left\langle\sigma,\, \frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial\theta_j}\right\rangle = 0, \quad \forall\, 1 \le j \le n-1.
$$

Therefore

$$
A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & G \end{bmatrix}
\implies |\det A|^2 = \det(A^T A) = |\det G|,
$$

so $|\det A| = \sqrt{|\det G|}$, exactly as needed. The volume element now simplifies cleanly without any messy determinant expansion:

$$
dx = r^{n-1}\sqrt{|\det G|}\; dr\,d\theta_1\cdots d\theta_{n-1} = r^{n-1}\,dr\,d\sigma.
$$

### Deriving the Formula

Plugging back into the original integral:

$$
I = \int_0^\infty\int_{\mathbb{S}^{n-1}} r^{n-1} e^{-r^2}\,d\sigma\,dr = |\mathbb{S}^{n-1}|\int_0^\infty r^{n-1} e^{-r^2}\,dr.
$$

One more substitution $t = r^2$ with $dt = 2r\,dr$ reveals the Gamma function:

$$
I = \frac{|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}|}{2}\int_0^\infty t^{(n-2)/2} e^{-t}\,dt = \frac{|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}|}{2}\,\Gamma(n/2).
$$

Comparing both methods gives

$$
\boxed{|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}| = \frac{2\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2)}}.
$$

---

## Computing $|B(0,1)|$

With the surface area in hand, the volume of the unit ball follows almost immediately — just apply the definition and switch to spherical coordinates:

$$
|B(0,1)| = \int_{B(0,1)} dx = \int_0^1\int_{\mathbb{S}^{n-1}} r^{n-1}\,d\sigma\,dr = |\mathbb{S}^{n-1}|\int_0^1 r^{n-1}\,dr = \frac{|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}|}{n}.
$$

Using the identity $\Gamma(n/2+1) = \frac{n}{2}\,\Gamma(n/2)$, this simplifies to

$$
|B(0,1)| = \frac{1}{n}\cdot\frac{2\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2)} = \frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\frac{n}{2}\,\Gamma(n/2)}
$$

$$
\boxed{|B(0,1)| = \frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2+1)}}.
$$

## Applications

The reason we need to compute the measure of balls in high-dimensional space is that it appears everywhere in analysis. In measure theory, spherical partitions are sometimes more flexible and easier to work with than cubical ones — I will present those problems later. In probability and statistics, the Gaussian distribution

$$
\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{n/2}}e^{-\|x\|^2/2}
$$

is an essential consequence of exactly the kind of computation done above. The result also appears in differential geometry via the Gauss-Bonnet formula, in isoperimetric inequalities, and in many other places I will get to in due course.

That wraps up the computation of the measure of balls in $\mathbb{R}^n$. For practice, here are some exercises.

## Exercises

 **Exercise 1.** Without any change of coordinates, define the number $\pi$ as an integral involving the functions $y = \sqrt{1-x^2}$ and $y = -\sqrt{1-x^2}$.

 **Exercise 2.** Compute $|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}|$ and $|B(0,1)|$ explicitly for $n = 1, 2, 3, 4$ and verify against the geometric formulas you already know. Hint: for $n = 1$, what is $\mathbb{S}^0$?

 **Exercise 3.** Prove that
 $$
 \Gamma\!\left(\frac{2k+1}{2}\right) = \frac{(2k-1)!!}{2^k}\sqrt{\pi}, \quad k \in \mathbb{N},
 $$
 where $(2k-1)!! = 1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdots (2k-1)$ is the double factorial.

 **Exercise 4.** Prove that the unit ball $B(0,1)$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is Lebesgue measurable.

 **Exercise 5.** Prove that $\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi}$ using differentiation under the integral sign. Specifically, consider
 $$
 F(t) = \left(\int_0^t e^{-x^2}\,dx\right)^2, \quad G(t) = \int_0^1 \frac{e^{-t^2(1+s^2)}}{1+s^2}\,ds
 $$
 and prove that $F(t) + G(t) = \pi/4$ for all $t \ge 0$.

 **Exercise 6.** Let $\omega_n = |B(0,1)|$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Prove the recurrence
 $$
 \omega_n = \omega_{n-2} \cdot \frac{2\pi}{n}, \quad \omega_1 = 2,\quad \omega_2 = \pi,
 $$
 without using the Gamma function.

 **Exercise 7.** Prove that the ratio between the surface area of the sphere and the volume of the ball of radius $r$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ satisfies
 $$
 \frac{|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}(r)|}{|B(0,r)|} = \frac{n}{r}.
 $$
 Explain why this identity is equivalent to $\frac{d}{dr}|B(0,r)| = |\mathbb{S}^{n-1}(r)|$.

 **Exercise 8.** Prove that $|B(0,1)| \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. Moreover, find the dimension $n_0$ at which $|B(0,1)|$ achieves its maximum.

 **Exercise 9.** For fixed $\varepsilon > 0$, prove that
 $$
 \frac{|B(0,1) \setminus B(0,1-\varepsilon)|}{|B(0,1)|} = 1 - (1-\varepsilon)^n \to 1 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty.
 $$
