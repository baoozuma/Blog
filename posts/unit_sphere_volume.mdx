## The Question

What is the volume of the unit ball $B^n = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n : \|x\| \leq 1\}$?

In low dimensions we know:

$$
|B^1| = 2, \quad |B^2| = \pi, \quad |B^3| = \frac{4\pi}{3}
$$

The pattern is not obvious. The answer turns out to be

$$
|B^n| = \frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma\!\left(\frac{n}{2} + 1\right)}
$$

and the surface area of $S^{n-1} = \partial B^n$ is

$$
|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}| = \frac{2\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma\!\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)}
$$

## Why This Is Surprising

Two things deserve attention.

**The volume peaks and then decays to zero.** Despite the ball growing in every direction, $|B^n| \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. Most of the volume of a high-dimensional ball concentrates near its equator — a phenomenon called *concentration of measure* that underlies much of modern probability and functional analysis.

**The Gaussian trick.** The cleanest derivation runs through the identity

$$
\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} e^{-\|x\|^2} \, dx = \pi^{n/2}
$$

Converting to spherical coordinates gives both formulas at once, via the Gamma function evaluated at half-integers.

## What We Will Prove

This post gives two proofs:

**Proof 1 (recursive).** Express $|B^n|$ in terms of $|B^{n-2}|$ by slicing along one axis and integrating. This gives a clean recursion but requires tracking the Gamma function carefully.

**Proof 2 (Gaussian integral).** Compute $\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} e^{-\|x\|^2} dx$ in two ways — as a product of 1D integrals, and in polar coordinates. The comparison yields the formula directly.

Along the way we derive the surface area formula and verify it against the known cases in dimensions 1, 2, 3.
